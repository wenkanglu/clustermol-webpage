Project Description


Molecular dynamics (MD) simulates the behaviour of molecules which are allowed to interact with one another over a fixed period of time; it outputs what is known as a trajectory of the behaviour observed. This trajectory is a record of the movement of every atom during the simulation, with respective changes in position over time calculated by solving Newtonโs equations of motion. Since the effects of molecular interactions occur so rapidly, positions are often updated at the femtosecond timescale. Therefore, trajectories containing even just a few nanoseconds worth of time frames amount to millions of locational data points to observe. MD simulations play an important role for researchers interested in the effects molecular conformations can have in their applications, some of which include drug-receptor interaction and protein folding. Consequently, it is imperative that effective methods exist to gather insight from trajectories as efficiently as possible. A common way to do so is through the use of clustering algorithms that can partition the trajectory into a set of dominant conformations that are deemed structurally unique from one another. This project looks to explore different clustering techniques to cluster flexible carbohydrate molecules like polysaccharides.


Project goals


We aim to investigate and compare the performance of the various clustering algorithms to identify which are effective in clustering trajectories of flexible molecules, specifically, carbohydrate molecules. In addition, we investigate the use of dimensionality reduction techniques on the trajectory data and whether this improves clustering results. An effective clustering algorithm is one that generates high-quality clusters with good partitioning. This means that the clusters have high intra-cluster similarity and low inter-cluster similarity. Intra-cluster similarity indicates how well a cluster is formed and how similar the objects within a specific cluster are. The inter-cluster similarity is used to determine how dissimilar different clusters are from one another. This is also referred to as cluster separation. We aim to address the following research question. Which clustering algorithms are most effective in clustering Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulation data of highly flexible molecules? Background and Context


Nicholas Limbert - HIERARCHICAL | QT

Clustering analysis provides a means to classify similar molecular conformations into a limited number of district groups thereby reducing the amount of information to be manually analysed. A wide variety of readily available clustering algorithms have not been implemented and tested on highly flexible molecules such as carbohydrates.

Hierarchical clustering is a well-developed clustering technique with numerous resources available. We utilise four different linkage criteria for hierarchical clustering - single, complete, average and Wardโs method. These are standard, widely available methods that provide an intuitive understanding of how clusters are formed.

The Quality Threshold (QT) algorithm aims to generate high-quality clusters by iteratively adding observations to a cluster while not exceeding the pre-defined cluster diameter or threshold. Observations are added to minimise the cluster diameter. The Quality Threshold (QT) algorithm has previously been implemented for clustering a range of MD data, however, there are some inconsistencies related to certain implementations. We, therefore, aim to make use of the implementation from Gonzaฬlez-Alemaฬn et al. which is in line with the originally proposed algorithm from Heyer et al. In addition, we make use of a vectorised version by Melvin et al in order to understand their differences and determine the effectiveness.

System Design and Overview


The system was built using two seperate databases: Neo4j, a graph database management system that has been shown to efficiently manage highly-connected data, and MySQL, a relational database. Degree violations were determined using a constraint-checking graph in Neo4j and if-statements in MySQL, while the performance of similar students was calculated using graph similarity algorithms and SQL queries in each database respectively.


Check the Basic Constraints of a Degree Plan

The constraint-checking graph consisted of question nodes which checked the various constraints of the degree and terminal nodes which classified the studentโs plan as suitable or unsuitable for a computer science degree. By traversing through question nodes such as โDo these courses constitute enough credits for the first year of studies?โ connected to other question and terminal nodes by โYesโ and โNoโ edges, errors in the studentโs curriculum were found.

Report the Performance of Similar Students

Student and academic data were stored in nodes used for similarity queries. These queries found students who are similar to one another by looking at their course enrollments and marks. The average course mark (accompanied by standard deviation) achieved by the clusters of similar students was then reported as an indication of how similar students fared in the past. Student nodes were connected to course nodes via ENROLLED-IN edges (with the obtained mark as an attribute). Course nodes were connected to major nodes through REQUIRES edges to store which courses are compulsory for a major.

Experimentation and Comparison with an SQL Implementation

To establish the suitability of Neo4j for this system, comparisons were made against an SQL implementation of the same data. The execution time of the similarity queries on the student data were compared to SQL queries in relational tables. These comparisons provided evidence to the viability of graph systems for this system and confirmed performance advantages Neo4j has over MySQL.

Testing with different database sizes

Initial testing was conducted on a small set of real UCT student data (3,000 students). To explore different graph database designs and retrieve more reliable performance results, new databases were created containing the data of 1,000,000 simulated students. The schema-less nature of Neo4j allowed for a new tree structure to be implemented in the larger graph database to improve the performance of similarity queries. MySQL did not support such a design and the data remained in tables. The tree structure implemented consisted of Student Root nodes which represented the curriculum shared by many students. All Student nodes which are children of Student Roots shared the same curriculum (represented by a curriculum hash). Instead of finding similar students by searching all student nodes, the search space is reduced by only evaluating Student Roots. Speed tests on these larger databases yielded more interesting results about the advantages of representing data graphically.

Due to the scope of the project, development was broken up into modules and protocols that could function as standalone programs. These programs would then function with the help of any relevant, previously developed modules that contributed to it. The staging of the final productโs development was broken down into three distinct stages:


Stage 1 - Parser and storage protocols

Stage 1 focused on creating the building blocks of the system, achieving the basic aims set out by the project sponsors and creating integration points for these building blocks, allowing them to be connected when integrating these blocks into tools developed in the future. This included developing protocols to parse raw Bayesian Network files, encode this data to summarized versions and finally storing all of this data in a Neo4j database.

Stage 2 - Generic system

Stage 2 focused on developing a general system that while not meeting the requirements of the stretch goal of the system, i.e. being a system tailored towards use with CS lecturers and potentially students, it did provide a general proof of concept for a Neo4j Bayesian Network powered system, as well as providing all the building blocks for future iterations. Meaning that any future studies into the use of Bayesian Network powered tools could utilise the stage 2 artefact as a basis for their code and simply customise it to their needs.

Users using the generic system, users are able to

  • Upload a raw .DNE Bayesian model file to be stored in the Neo4j database using the relevant parsing, encoding and storage protocols
  • View all models already stored within the generic systems library
  • Choose a model from the library and perform inference using this model by providing a set of model specific evidence

Results/ Conclusions


Multi-data point linkage criteria produce better results compared to single linkage criteria.

Hierarchical clustering is unable to classify noise separately, however, it is still able to produce clusters with relatively good CVI values. A limitation to Hierarchical clustering is that it requires prior knowledge about the number of conformations within a simulation. This information is usually not available unless prior research has been conducted on the specified data set. As expected, single linkage is unable to produce clusters of significance and merges most frames into one cluster. Complete, however can produce reasonable results given that only individual data points are taken into account when merging clusters. Complete also has better CVI values and a greater cluster count spread to those produced by single linkage. Wardโs method and average linkage produce much better results in comparison to single and complete linkages. Linkage criteria (Wardโs and average) that take multiple data points into account produce significantly better results and should be implemented when using Hierarchical clustering.

Quality Threshold produces good clusters for highly flexible molecules.

Quality Threshold produces good clusters for highly flexible molecules as is evident from the results obtained for S. flexneri, MenW and to an extent MenY. Notably, results are only informative once the correct cutoff value has been found. Lower cut-off values impose a stricter constraint on the QT variations and produce more noise data. A balance between clusters produced and noise should be determined. Minimising noise by increasing the cut-off value leads to larger clusters containing dissimilar conformations.

It is evident that both QT variants can cluster highly flexible molecules and can produce high-quality clusters with minimal noise albeit the correct cut-off values have to be determined. Selection of a cut-off value should be based on iteratively evaluating different CVIโs while changing the cut-off values. One should evaluate the noise and clusters produced to determine the most suitable cut-off value.This further attests to QTโs existing popularity within the domain of clustering MD data.
